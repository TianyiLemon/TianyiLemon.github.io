The Probabilistic Method: A Gentle Introduction

January 2025 · 12 min read

The probabilistic method is one of the most beautiful proof techniques in combinatorics. It allows us to prove that objects with certain properties exist without ever constructing them explicitly. This note introduces the basic ideas through Erdős's elegant proof that Ramsey numbers grow exponentially.

The Basic Idea

Suppose we want to prove that an object with property $P$ exists in some collection $\mathcal{C}$. The probabilistic method works as follows: define a probability distribution over $\mathcal{C}$, and show that a random object has property $P$ with positive probability. If $\Pr[X \text{ has property } P] > 0$, then such an object must exist!

This might seem almost tautological, but its power lies in the fact that computing probabilities is often much easier than explicit construction.

Ramsey Numbers

Let's recall the definition. The Ramsey number $R(k, k)$ is the smallest $n$ such that any 2-coloring of the edges of $K_n$ contains a monochromatic $K_k$.

Ramsey's theorem tells us that $R(k,k)$ is finite for all $k$. But how fast does it grow? The upper bound from the standard proof gives $R(k,k) \le \binom{2k-2}{k-1} \le 4^k$. Can we do better? Is there a clever coloring that avoids monochromatic cliques?

Theorem (Erdős, 1947). For all $k \ge 3$, we have $R(k,k) > 2^{k/2}$.

This was revolutionary—it showed that the exponential growth is necessary, not just an artifact of a weak proof.

The Proof

We'll show that for $n = \lfloor 2^{k/2} \rfloor$, there exists a 2-coloring of $K_n$ with no monochromatic $K_k$. Consider a random coloring: each edge is colored red or blue independently with probability $1/2$ each.

Fix a set $S$ of $k$ vertices. What's the probability that $S$ forms a monochromatic clique?

The number of edges in $K_k$ is $\binom{k}{2}$. For $S$ to be monochromatic red, all $\binom{k}{2}$ edges must be red, which happens with probability $2^{-\binom{k}{2}}$. The same holds for blue. By the union bound:

$$ \Pr[S \text{ is monochromatic}] = 2 \cdot 2^{-\binom{k}{2}} = 2^{1 - \binom{k}{2}} $$

Now we take a union bound over all $\binom{n}{k}$ possible $k$-subsets:

$$ \Pr[\exists \text{ monochromatic } K_k] \le \binom{n}{k} \cdot 2^{1-\binom{k}{2}} $$

Using $\binom{n}{k} \le n^k / k!$ and $\binom{k}{2} = k(k-1)/2$, we get:

$$ \Pr[\exists \text{ monochromatic } K_k] \le \frac{n^k}{k!} \cdot 2^{1 - k(k-1)/2} $$

Substituting $n = 2^{k/2}$:

$$ \frac{(2^{k/2})^k}{k!} \cdot 2^{1-k(k-1)/2} = \frac{2^{k^2/2} \cdot 2^{1-k(k-1)/2}}{k!} = \frac{2^{(k+2)/2}}{k!} $$

For $k \ge 3$, we have $k! > 2^{(k+2)/2}$, so this probability is less than 1. Therefore, with positive probability, a random coloring has no monochromatic $K_k$. Such a coloring must exist. $\square$

What Makes This Remarkable

Erdős's proof tells us that "most" colorings work, yet we cannot explicitly write down a single one! For $k = 10$, we know $R(10,10) > 32$, but no one has ever exhibited a specific 2-coloring of $K_{32}$ avoiding monochromatic $K_{10}$.

This existential-but-non-constructive nature troubled some mathematicians initially. But the method has proven so powerful that it's now a cornerstone of combinatorics, with applications ranging from coding theory to geometry.

Beyond the Basics

The simple union bound argument above is just the beginning. More sophisticated techniques include:

  • Expectation method: Show $\E[X] > 0$ for some random variable counting good objects
  • Alteration: Start with a random object and "fix" bad parts
  • Lovász Local Lemma: Handle dependencies between bad events
  • Entropy compression: Use information-theoretic arguments

I'll cover the Lovász Local Lemma in a future post—it's the tool you reach for when events aren't independent but are "mostly" independent.

Further Reading: Alon and Spencer's The Probabilistic Method is the definitive reference. For a gentler introduction, see Chapter 5 of Matoušek and Nešetřil's Invitation to Discrete Mathematics.
